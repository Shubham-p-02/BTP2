INGE THULIN: Yes, well first of all, we see improved productivity coming out of the laboratories, which we're very pleased about. DEANE DRAY: That's great color. My second question is -- I wanted to go back to the hedging practices to Nick for moment. But I was curious why would you not increase the percent of your current hedges from 50% to let's say 75%? Did you look at that and what the costs are there?
NICK GANGESTAD: Yes, Deane, thanks for asking a clarifying question on that. One of the constraints or governors on our ability to hedge is qualifying for hedge accounting treatment. And, if we go over 50% in most cases, we would then be subject to mark-to-market accounting and, therefore, defeat the purpose of what we're trying to do with our hedging process. In fact, we'd probably add more volatility if we did that. So that's the upper-limit constraint on what we can.
And part of our approach, if I go back over a decade, is not to eliminate all the risks there. Our approach is to give the business some time to react and adjust to the changing business conditions. And that's consistent with our 50% strategy.
DEANE DRAY: Great, Nick, that clarification was real helpful. We would rather not see you go through that -- the currency mark-to-market gyrations. So we'll live with the 50% then. Thanks.
